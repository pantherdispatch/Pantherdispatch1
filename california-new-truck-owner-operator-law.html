California New Truck Owner Operator Law | All You Need To Know

The certiorari on California owner-operator law, aka AB5, was denied by the U.S. Supreme Court on June 30, 2022. Because of this, the injunction on one of the most infamous California trucking laws, AB5, will be lifted, and the law will be implemented in California retroactively to January 1, 2020.

Overview of AB5

Assembly Bill 5 (AB5) was passed by the California State Legislature and signed by Governor Gavin Newsom in September 2019. The law reclassifies a lot of workers as employees instead of independent contractors.

AB5 has a three-point ABC test, and a worker is considered an employee except if they meet the following three-point criteria

  • 1 - The worker is entirely free of the direction and control of the hiring person when they're performing their work.
  • 2 - The worker's work does not involve the usual course of the hiring entity.
  • 3 - The worker is generally engaged in independently established trade, business, or occupation of the exact nature of work done for the hirer.

The AB5 California owner-operator law is not the only one with the ABC test. These kinds of tests are used in other states to classify workers. However, this one is the hardest one to pass.

Does AB5 Include Trucking Companies?

The bill was initially intended for the drive-hailing and delivery companies like Uber and DoorDash to classify their drivers as employees, so they can be entitled to various employee benefits. However, the law got a broader scope and included the trucking and logistic companies to reclassify their workers as employees.

In short, YES! The AB5 law includes trucking companies, and it was announced by the Supreme Court that no case challenging the legislation would be heard.

Does AB5 Ban Owner-operators?

The short answer is no. The AB5 doesn't ban owner-operators. However, it makes it extremely hard for trucking or logistic companies to use them. The B clause of the ABC test says that the worker should perform tasks outside the usual course of the hirer to be assumed as an independent contractor rather than an employee.

As most of the owner-operators work in contracts with shipping and logistic companies, the B point of the test is almost impossible to prove.

What's the Big Deal with AB5 Including Trucking Companies?

AB5 could essentially revamp the way businesses are carried out by drivers or carriers in California. Moreover, it is expected that the law will gradually include other states. Motor carriers in California are now obligated to either reclassify all their drivers as employees or change how the two parties interact.

As employees, trucking companies must provide employee benefits to their drivers, which makes it difficult for the companies to use owner-operators.

The bill also affects drivers as they now have less flexibility in choosing their work. As California is the home to the busiest port in North America, what happens in California is usually expected to spread to the rest of the country.

Which Drivers are Most Impacted by AB5?

There are over 70,000 owner-operators in California that are affected by AB5. However, the most intense effect is faced by a smaller group of these drivers. Drivers for a range of transportation companies like ride-hailing or even trucking companies may be reclassified as employees rather than independent contractors if not proven by the three-point test explained above.

Are Truckers Supporting the New California Owner-operator Law?

The basic intent behind AB5 was to protect the workers from exploitation by management, and it seems like all the drivers would support the court decision. However, things are not as simple as they seem.

Like the rest of the country, California's trucking market is vast and fragmented. Trucking companies come in all shapes and sizes, and each one has its own unique business model. Not all drivers will be affected similarly and probably have different points of view.

Various drivers and smaller trucking companies have joined forces with larger motor carriers to oppose the law, arguing for greater autonomy and flexibility.

What Can Trucking Companies Do About AB5?

As AB5 is in action now, here are a few options that may be followed by the trucking companies in order to tackle the situation:

Shift your business from California

Taking this step is not necessarily beneficial for either motor carriers or drivers. However, this can be done as a drastic step to avoid legal conflicts.

Switch to the employer-employee model

You can simply switch to the employer-employee model for your business, which means creating a labor environment so that all the full-time drivers can benefit from the employee benefits.

Create separate brokerage and carrier business

Creating two distinct businesses can be a good strategy for dealing with the situation. One business should employ drivers directly, and the other can act as a broker for independent contractors who are empowered to set their own rates and accept work from other sources.

What Can Drivers Do About AB5?

Here are a few options that can help drivers, mainly leased owner-operators, with the situation:

Move out of the state

Drivers can simply shift their residence outside California or run over 50% of their miles outside California.

Change their status by getting authority

By getting their own operating authority, drivers can drive for a brokerage instead of a leased owner-operator.

Avoid picking outbound loads

After driving freight into the state, drivers could deadhead out of California.

Conclusion

With the AB5 California owner-operator law in action, both drivers and motor carriers now have to change the way they function in their business. The Supreme court's decision can seem more frustrating for big companies than drivers, but this is not true. Some drivers and owner-operators have shifted their residences out of California as the law hinders them from working independently.
